The verb 'be'

Key points: existence, state of being, main forms, uses, questions, negatives, contractions

1. Basic Meaning

"Be" expresses existence or a state of being. It tells us that something or someone exists or has a certain quality.

2. Main Forms

  • am (I)
  • is (he/she/it)
  • are (you/we/they)
  • was (I/he/she/it)
  • were (you/we/they)

3. Uses

  • To describe someone or something: "She is tall."
  • To state where something is: "The book is on the table."
  • To tell the time: "It is 3 o'clock."
  • To talk about someone's job: "I am a teacher."
  • In passive sentences: "The house was built in 1990."

4. Helping Verb

"Be" is also used as an auxiliary verb to form continuous tenses and passive voice.

  • "I am working." (present continuous)
  • "The car is being repaired." (passive voice)

5. Questions

Form questions by putting "be" before the noun/pronoun:

  • "Is she happy?"
  • "Are you a student?"
  • "Is it raining?"

6. Negative Form

Add "not" after the verb "be":

  • "I am not tired."
  • "She is not here."

7. Contractions

Contractions are common in spoken English and informal writing. Here are the rules for using contractions with 'be':

Common Contractions

  • I'm (I am), you're (you are), he's (he is), she's (she is), it's (it is), we're (we are), they're (they are)
  • isn't (is not), aren't (are not), wasn't (was not), weren't (were not)

Rules for Using Contractions

  1. Using more than one contraction is not allowed:
    • Correct: "She's not ready."
    • Incorrect: ~~"She'sn't ready."~~
  2. Affirmative contractions are not used at the end of clauses:
    A: "I think they're late."
    B: "Yes, I think they are." (Not: ~~"I think they're."~~)
  3. Negative contractions can be used at the end of clauses:
    A: "Is the meeting still on for today?"
    B: "No, it isn't."
  4. Contractions are commonly used in tag questions:
    • "He's the new manager, isn't he?"
  5. In question forms, 'am not' is contracted to 'aren't':
    • Correct: "I'm doing well in this class, aren't I?"
    • Incorrect: ~~"I'm doing well in this class, amn't I?"~~

Example Conversation

A: "Are you ready for the presentation?"
B: "No, I'm not quite ready yet. I'm still reviewing my notes."
A: "Oh, I see. You're presenting first, aren't you?"
B: "Yes, I am. I'm a bit nervous."
