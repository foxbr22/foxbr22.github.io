Possessive Adjectives

Key points: definition, forms, usage, examples, comparison with possessive pronouns

1. What are Possessive Adjectives?

Possessive adjectives are words that show ownership or belonging. They are used before nouns to indicate who or what the noun belongs to.

2. Forms of Possessive Adjectives

Here's a table comparing subject pronouns, possessive adjectives, and possessive pronouns:

Subject Pronoun Possessive Adjective Possessive Pronoun
I my mine
you your yours
he his his
she her hers
it its -
we our ours
they their theirs

Note: "It" does not have a possessive pronoun form. We use "its" only as a possessive adjective.

3. Usage

Possessive adjectives are placed before the noun they modify. They do not change based on the noun's number (singular or plural).

Example: My books, not books my

4. Examples in Sentences

  • This is my car.
  • What's your name?
  • He forgot his keys.
  • She loves her job.
  • The cat licked its paw.
  • We need to clean our room.
  • They lost their way in the forest.

5. Important Notes

  • Don't confuse "its" (possessive) with "it's" (contraction of "it is").
  • Possessive adjectives are different from possessive pronouns (mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs).

Possessive pronouns are used to replace the noun that is being referred to, while possessive adjectives are used before the noun. For example:

  • Possessive adjective: This is my book.
  • Possessive pronoun: This book is mine.

Possessive pronouns can stand alone in a sentence, whereas possessive adjectives always need to be followed by a noun.

  • The possessive adjective agrees with the owner, not with the thing that is owned.
  • "It" is an exception: it only has a possessive adjective form ("its") and no possessive pronoun form.
