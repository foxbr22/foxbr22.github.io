Do, Have, and Make - Idiomatic Expressions

Some idiomatic expressions can be difficult for students who are learning English because the words can be used with different meanings. Let's talk about some common expressions using the verbs "do," "have," and "make."

1. Do

Use "do" for repetitive general tasks or activities that don't create something new. Examples:

  • I need to do the laundry (cleaning clothes).
  • Today is your day to do the dishes (cleaning the dishes).
  • Daniel, have you done your homework?
  • I can't go to the party; I have work to do.

2. Have

Use "have" when talking about experiences, relationships, meals, events, and illnesses. Examples:

  • We're going to have a good time!
  • I'm not lying! I have a girlfriend.
  • We had (past of "have") eggs for breakfast.
  • I am having a party this weekend.
  • I can't go; I have a fever.

3. Make

Use "make" when creating or preparing something, such as meals, drinks, or plans. Examples:

  • Don't bring McDonald's; I'm making dinner.
  • I made (past of "make") a Caipirinha for you.
  • I made some plans for the weekend.
  • The first thing I do when I wake up is make my bed. *

*Be careful! In the last example, the person is not creating a bed; they are just organizing it. This is an exception. "Make the bed" is a common idiomatic expression in English. Although it is a repetitive task, the proper expression is "making the bed," not "doing the bed."
