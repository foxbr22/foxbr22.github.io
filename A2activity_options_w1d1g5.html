Possessive Pronouns and 'Whose'

Key points: possessive pronouns, usage, comparison with possessive adjectives, 'whose' as a question word and relative pronoun

1. Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns show ownership. They replace a noun + possessive adjective combination.

Subject Pronoun Possessive Adjective Possessive Pronoun
I my mine
you your yours
he his his
she her hers
it its -
we our ours
they their theirs

2. Usage of Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns are used to replace a noun and show possession. They stand alone and are not followed by a noun.

This is my book. → This book is mine.
That is her car. → That car is hers.
Whose car is this? → It's hers.

Notice how possessive pronouns help avoid repeating nouns:

Whose car is this?
It's hers.

In this example, 'hers' is used instead of 'her car' in the response.

3. Possessive Pronouns vs. Possessive Adjectives

Unlike possessive adjectives, possessive pronouns can stand alone in a sentence. They don't need to be followed by a noun.

Possessive Adjectives:

  • Always come before a noun
  • Cannot stand alone in a sentence
This is my book. (correct)
This is my. (incorrect)

Possessive Pronouns:

  • Replace the noun + possessive adjective
  • Can stand alone in a sentence
This book is mine. (correct)
Is this yours? (correct)

4. The Word 'Whose'

'Whose' has two main uses:

  1. As a question word to ask about possession
  2. As a relative pronoun in relative clauses

4.1 'Whose' as a Question Word

We use 'whose' to ask about possession or ownership.

Whose book is this?
Whose turn is it?

4.2 'Whose' as a Relative Pronoun

'Whose' can also be used to give more information about a person or thing, showing possession.

The woman whose car was stolen is very upset.
I know a boy whose father is a famous actor.

5. Important Notes

  • Don't confuse 'whose' (possessive) with 'who's' (contraction of 'who is' or 'who has').
  • 'It' doesn't have a possessive pronoun form.
